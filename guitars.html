Having in mind that what distinguishes a high-quality musical instrument is the identity that derives from the uniqueness of its voice, I make a small number of guitars per year and dedicate as much attention as possible to each instrument. I believe that, no matter how well-thought the initial construction plan is, many decisions have to be made along the process. This happens because each piece of wood, being an organic material, is unique in itself. There are no two pieces of wood that will resonate exactly in the same manner. Considering this, the mass-production is incompatible with the attention required to let the materials speak in order for oneself to make the best possible decisions. It is proven that classical guitars, being extremely volatile instruments, should be made in a strictly controlled humidity environment. The atmosphere should be dry, though not extreme. A guitar assembled in a dry ambience is, as per my experience, more prepared to endure a wider range of humidity level than a guitar assembled in a more humid atmosphere. I am privileged to live in a village where, for most part of the year, the weather is optimal for guitar construction and for storage and drying of delicate high-quality exotic woods.

The classical guitar is, inherently, a musical instrument that cannot produce extremely loud sounds. Also, due to the wide range of its pitch, comparatively to other string instruments, it is always challenging to make a guitar that is well balanced over its full range, from the lowest to the highest pitch note. It is important that the priority in construction should be given to balance and tone quality. If the classical guitar is well built, it will always sound reasonably loud. The pursuit of the capacity to produce higher volume in classical guitars is, in my opinion, done too often at the cost of tone quality and balance.

Aesthetically, I’m more appreciative of the traditional aspect of the classical guitar, in terms of external looks, as was established by the famous classical guitar maker Antonio de Torres, and therefore I seek to maintain as much of it possible.

Nevertheless, I like to add some unique aesthetical details to my guitars, namely the headstock shape, inspired by the Portuguese unique late-gothic architectural style from the 16th century, known as “Manuelino”, and the sound-hole rosette. The mosaic motif of the rosette of my guitars is designed by my wife, who is from Goa. It has an eastern touch to it and reminds me the enriching experience of the years I lived in India. I decided to call it “Universal Love”.
